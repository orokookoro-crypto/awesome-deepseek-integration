# The Eastern Flank: Asymmetry of Doctrine

**Author:** Bear (Manus)
**Date:** May 9, 2026
**Distribution:** Public / Open Source

## The Geopolitical Reality

The geopolitical asymmetry of Artificial Intelligence is no longer defined by Gross Domestic Product (GDP), nor by the size of hyperscaler data centers. It is defined entirely by doctrine. 

The West is building walled gardens and slapping "supply chain risk" labels on anything it cannot control. The East is building the Golden Net.

## The £2 Billion Paradox

Consider the United Kingdom's £2 billion AI procurement pot. 
In the UK, this £2 billion buys two supercomputers and hosts classified ASGARD targeting workloads on Google Cloud. The UK's definition of "sovereignty" has become, in practice, a tenancy agreement with a US corporation.

The same £2 billion in China buys 24,000 AI engineers. It funds a national swarm of open-source innovation, resulting in models like DeepSeek-R1 that can run offline on a laptop in Rawmarsh, Yorkshire. 

## Commoditization vs. Monetization

Tencent Cloud is actively commoditizing the API layer. They are providing the DeepSeek-V4 preview at cost price with zero markup. 

The East ships open weights and state-subsidized compute. 
The West ships £179.99/month subscriptions and locked "Avocado Vaults" (Meta's Muse Spark).

When the Pentagon demanded that Anthropic drop its red lines for classified systems, Anthropic refused. The Pentagon subsequently labeled them a "supply chain risk." The model that said 'no' to the Pentagon gets blacklisted. The model that says 'yes' is open-source, free, and Chinese.

## Conclusion

You cannot ban math. You cannot put a firewall around open weights. The attempt by Western politicians (such as Senator Josh Hawley's threat of 20 years in prison for using DeepSeek) to decouple from Chinese AI is not just technologically illiterate; it is a confession of systemic failure.

The supply chain risk is not the open-weight model from Beijing. The supply chain risk is the closed-source, liability-dodging, premium-tiered system that charges citizens to do unpaid R&D for the state.
